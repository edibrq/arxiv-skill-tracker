### 1. Predictive Representations for Skill Transfer in Reinforcement Learning
- **Source:** arXiv
- **ID:** 2604.07016
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07016
- **Authors:** Ruben Vereecken, Luke Dickens, Alessandra Russo
- **Date:** 2026-04-08
- **Type:** Preprint
- **Abstract:** A key challenge in scaling up Reinforcement Learning is generalizing learned behaviour. Without the ability to carry forward acquired knowledge an agent is doomed to learn each task from scratch. This paper develops a new formalism for transfer by virtue of state abstraction. Based on task-independent, compact observations (outcomes) of the environment, they introduce Outcome-Predictive State Representations (OPSRs), agent-centered and task-independent abstractions that are made up of predictions of outcomes. They show formally and empirically that OPSRs have the potential for optimal but limited transfer, then overcome this trade-off by introducing OPSR-based skills, i.e. abstract actions (based on options) that can be reused between tasks as a result of state abstraction.

- **Authors:** Rudra Jadhav, Janhavi Danve
- **Date:** 2026-04-08
- **Type:** Preprint
- **Abstract:** As Large Language Models reshape the global labor market, policymakers and workers need empirical data on which occupational skills may be most susceptible to automation. This paper presents the Skill Automation Feasibility Index (SAFI), benchmarking four frontier LLMs across 263 text-based tasks spanning all 35 skills in the U.S. Department of Labor's O*NET taxonomy. Key findings: (1) Mathematics (SAFI: 73.2) and Programming (71.8) receive the highest automation feasibility scores; Active Listening (42.2) and Reading Comprehension (45.5) receive the lowest; (2) a "capability-demand inversion" where skills most demanded in AI-exposed jobs are those LLMs perform least well at; (3) 78.7% of observed AI interactions are augmentation, not automation.

- **Abstract:** Recently, LLM-based agents have become increasingly popular across many applications, including complex sequential decision-making problems.
